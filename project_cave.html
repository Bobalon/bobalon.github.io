Cave Critters
  • Genre: Puzzle-Strategy
  • Team size: 6

MY ROLE

  • Responsible for Mechanics Design
  • Designed the Pause Menu
  • Collaborated on other Menus
  • Researched and sketched out basic Level Design and suggestions for UI
  • Collaborated with the whole group on UX

Cave Critters is a puzzle game in the vein of Lemmings, except you draw lines with a mouse instead of assigning roles and powers.

This project has gone through a lot of changes and I've produced about 16 pages worth of explanatory drawings throughout. We were limited to store-bought 3D assets and it had to have Fantasy themes and some kind of network functionality. Below are my final explanatory drawings for controls and basic gameplay, as they appear in the Pause Menu in-game.

The team was made up of 3 Designers and 3 Programmers. The first thing we did was, as always, brainstorming and pitching to each other. We were all in the mood for something non-violent, which is reflected in my suggestions.

The Painting Game caught the imagination of the group and we immediately started discussing what it could include and how to keep the scope manageable. We decided to give the world a 2.5D perspective, to keep movement and collision simple.

I took inspiration from Passpartout: The Starving Artist and an old Flash game called Avant Garde (about modernist painters and isms) and drew a feature wishlist. I also started exploring UI ideas.

At this point, the Programmers pulled the brakes. To avoid feature creep, we tried to orient the design more completely around the drawing mechanic itself. We started talking about a puzzle platformer built around drawing lines to overcome obstacles.

We put together a small list of potential game and media influences and I drew some basic gameplay ideas.

We elaborated on the initial Design. I tried to keep it simple enough that you could control it entirely with the mouse. There was some discussion about whether we should have proper levels or opt for an endless runner. I designed a tutorial level for testing.

Testing quickly revealed one problem - it's difficult to draw with a moving camera. I drew 3 suggested solutions: section the level into static screens, hold the middle mouse button to move the camera, or the camera follows your mouse pointer by default and you press a button to toggle a "drawing mode" where it becomes static.

In the end, none of them were implemented and we fell back on controlling the camera with WASD.

We started exploring the idea of a Hub area, both to empower the Player to choose their path through the game, but also to have a more natural space for dialogue and narrative. I especially like how we managed to get the same run-and-draw gameplay to work in the more sedate setting of the Hub World, by initiating conversation when you literally run into people.

We also realized that abandoning the initial concept had brought us back to square one in terms of network functionality, so I drew a few ideas for that as well.

I drew some more level ideas. Then I started thinking about Narrative in earnest. I figured since you never actually control the runner, we might as well make the Player themselves a sort of Character, who interacts with the world by drawing. I explored the idea of the runner being haunted by the Player. The style of the Character in these drawings is due to us deciding on a Miner prefab as our main character.

Most of the early Narrative work got scrapped, since we abandoned the single runner concept. The game grew closer to Lemmings, with a large number of runners on screen at the same time, and the Hub World was cut entirely.
We also fell back on 2D with 3D characters instead of 2.5D. Finally we gave up on having a working eraser and opted for lines disappearing after a set amount of time instead.

It's around this time that choose the name Cave Critters.

My final contributions were the Logo, a Poster, updating gameplay instructions for the Pause Menu, custom buttons for the Pause and Victory screens, and putting the Trailer together.
