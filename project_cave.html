Cave Critters
  • Genre: Puzzle-Strategy
  • Team size: 6

MY ROLE

  • Responsible for Mechanics Design
  • Designed the Pause Menu
  • Collaborated on other Menus

  • Collaborated with the whole group on UX

"Cave Critters" is a puzzle game reminiscent of Lemmings, except you draw lines with a mouse instead of assigning roles and powers.

The project went through a lot of changes and I produced about 16 pages worth of explanatory drawings throughout. We were limited to store-bought 3D assets and it had to have Fantasy themes. Below are my final explanatory drawings for controls and basic gameplay, as they appear in the Pause Menu in-game.

The team was made up of 3 Designers and 3 Programmers. We imagined a puzzle platformer built around drawing lines to overcome obstacles.

We put together a small list of game and media influences and I drew some gameplay ideas.

We elaborated on the initial Design. One of my goals was to keep it simple enough to control entirely with the mouse. There was some discussion about whether we should have proper levels or opt for an endless runner. I designed a tutorial level for testing.

Testing proved that it's difficult to draw with a moving camera. I drew 3 suggested solutions: section the level into static screens, hold the middle mouse button to move the camera, or the camera follows your mouse pointer by default and you press a button to toggle a "drawing mode" where it becomes static.

We eventually fell back on controlling the camera with WASD, due to ease of implementation.

We explored the idea of a Hub area, both to empower the Player to choose their path through the game, but also to have a more natural space for dialogue and narrative. I especially like how we managed to get the same run-and-draw gameplay to work in the more sedate setting of the Hub World, by initiating conversation when you literally run into people.

We also tried to develop some ideas for network functionality.

I drew some more level ideas. Then I started thinking about Narrative in earnest. I figured since you never actually control the runner, we might as well make the Player themselves a sort of Character, who interacts with the world by drawing. I explored the idea of the runner being haunted by the Player.

Most of the Narrative work got scrapped when we abandoned the single runner concept. The game grew closer to Lemmings, with a large number of runners on screen at the same time, and the Hub World was cut entirely.

As the deadline approached, we gave up on having a working eraser and opted for lines disappearing after a set amount of time instead.

My final contributions were the Logo, a Poster, updating gameplay instructions for the Pause Menu, custom buttons for the Pause and Victory screens, and putting the Trailer together.
