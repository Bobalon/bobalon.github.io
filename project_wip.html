MY ROLE

  • Responsible for Storyboards and Narrative

  • Collaborated with the whole group on UX

A puzzle game in the vein of Lemmings, except you draw lines with a mouse instead of assigning roles and powers. We were limited to store-bought 3D assets, it had to have Fantasy themes, and it needed to have a network element.

This project is still ongoing, and as such I have no final version or trailer to show. It has, however, gone through a lot of changes, and I've produced about 15-16 pages worth of explanatory drawings that may shed light on my process.

Below is the current state of the Miro board.

The team is made up of 3 Designers and 3 Programmers. The first thing we did was, as always, brainstorming and pitching to each other, then putting to to a vote. We were all, I think, looking for something largely non-violent, which is reflected in my suggestions.

The Painting Game idea caught the imagination of the group and we immediately started discussing what it could include and how to keep the scope manageable. We decided that the Fantasy theme meant the Painters would be supernatural creatures of some sort. We decided to give the world a 2.5D perspective, to keep movement and collision simple.

I took inspiration from Passpartout: The Starving Artist and an old Flash game called Avant Garde (about modernist painters and isms) and drew a kind of feature wishlist. I also started exploring UI ideas.

At this point, the Programmers pulled the brakes. In the interest of avoiding feature creep, we tried to focus the design, orient it more completely around the drawing mechanic itself. We started talking about a puzzle platformer built around drawing lines to overcome obstacles.

We put together a small list of potential game and media influences (along with links for games that were playable for free) and I drew some basic gameplay ideas.

We elaborated on the initial Design. I tried to keep it simple enough to be possible to control entirely with the mouse. There was some discussion about whether we should have proper levels or opt for an endless runner. I designed a preliminary tutorial level, for testing.

Testing quickly revealed one major problem - it's very difficult to draw with a moving camera. I drew 3 suggested solutions: section the level into static screens, hold middle mouse button to move the camera, or the camera follows your mouse pointer by default and you press a button to toggle a "drawing mode" where it becomes static.

Ideally they should all start with a full view of the level, then zoom in. We're currently implementing the first one.

We started exploring the idea of a Hub area, both to empower the Player to choose their path through the game, but also to have a more natural space for dialogue and narrative. I especially like how we managed to get the same run-and-draw gameplay to work in the more sedate setting of the Hub World, by initiating conversation when you literally run into people.

We also realized that abandoning the initial concept had brought us back to square one in terms of network functionality, so I drew a few ideas for that as well.

I drew some more level ideas and tried suggesting some paths through pickup placement. Then I started thinking about Narrative in earnest. I figure since you never actually control the runner, we might as well make the actual Player Character, the one who draws, vague and largely equivalent to the actual Player. I explored the idea of the runner being haunted by the Player.

Most of the early Narrative work is now scrapped, since we abandoned the single runner concept. The game is now closer to Lemmings, with a large number of runners on screen at the same time, and the Hub World is cut entirely.

This game is, as I said above, still being worked on. We've given up on implementing the eraser functionality in time, and we're abandoning pickups in favour of gradually recharging bars for the different types of Paint.
